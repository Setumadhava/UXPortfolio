The Problem

In India, water is provided to households in scheduled intervals. Most stand-alone homes use an underground sump tank to collect the government supplied water and pump that water into rooftop overhead tanks. This process is inefficient and involves uncertainties which causes inconvenience to residents.

A

Residents do not know how much water is left to use or how long to run the pump to refill it.

B

Residents are not sure how long to keep the pump running. If the switch is not turned off promptly, this results in tank overflow.

C

Residents do not know exactly when the government water will come as it varies seasonally.

Research

We used 5 research methods to understand the target users and the contextual details of managing water in their homes. We carried out interviews & contextual inquiry with users in India remotely through Skype calls.

Interviews

 Through the 3 interviews and 2 contextual inquiries we got a good understanding of how different people manage water at their homes. What surprised me the most was each person we interviewed had a unique setup in their homes to manage water.

Journey Map

Creating journey maps helped us to better understand the most important scenarios that users experienced while managing their water. The maps detail what users are thinking, feeling, and doing each step of the way. The map below describes the process of filling the overhead tank.



Why Journey Map?

 Initially our plan was to create user personas to inform our designs. However, when we interviewed people, we learned that every individual had a unique way of managing water depending on thier personal setup and habits, making it hard for us to find too many things in common to create a persona. Thus we decided we create a journey map instead which helped us better visualize the problem.

Key Research Findings

Ideation

We translated our research findings to a list of user needs related to the water management system in a stand-alone Indian home. We focused our ideation efforts to solve for these needs. Our team used the methods described below to arrive at a final concept.

Storyboards

Using the user needs for inspiration, the team came together and sketched potential solutions that would improve or enhance the user experience. We presented our concepts to each other, identifying how each sketch addressed one or more user needs. We critiqued the group’s sketches and identified strengths and weaknesses of the potential designs.

User Need Ranking

The research & storyboard process helped us narrow down to 12 user needs. In order to scope our solution, we asked users to rank the needs, in order of importance. We averaged the ranking scores, sorted them, and decided to prioritize the top six user needs when building our low fidelity prototype.

Concept For Prototyping

From our sketches and storyboards, the most comprehensive solution that could address the six prioritized needs required a water level sensor in the overhead tank and a Wi-Fi controlled pump switch. This IoT system would be paired with an Android app which would provide real-time information & control to users over their water systems. Also, from the research we learned that Android was the most popular mobile platform in India and hence we chose to design an Android app.

Low Fidelity Prototype & Usability Study

Using the final concept from our ideation phase, we created a low fidelity prototype of an Android app to address the top six most important user needs. We tested this prototype with five users and iterated on the prototype as users encountered issues.

Low Fidelity Prototype

Based on the sketches & storyboards, we designed our prototype app to accomplish five user tasks based off of the six prioritized user needs.

Usability Study

After creating a low fidelity app prototype, we tested it with five people. In order to quickly fix usability issues, we made design changes to our prototype after each test (RITE method). We also gathered information based on how useful or easy to use each feature was.

High-Fidelity Prototype

We incorporated our findings from our low fidelity usability study and created a final, high fidelity prototype of the Pump It Up mobile app. We added information about the sump tank level, created a way to fill the overhead tank by percentage, and made some smaller, polish and color changes to bring the app together.

Feature Description

Overhead and Sump Tank Info

Participants mentioned that knowing the sump tank level information is critical to fill the overhead tank. I added sump tank water level information in places where users need it to make a decision

Filling Tank Remotely

Through the app, users wanted to fill the tanks not just by time (eg 20 minutes of pump ON time), but also by tank level (Eg: 50% tank). This feature was incorporated in the high-fidelity designs.

Water Supply Schedule & Water News

Knowing the government water supply schedule & regional water news was one of the top priorities for users.

Future Work

Our team had additional ideas or heard suggestions from user testing that were out of scope for this project. We believe these could be explored in future iterations.

Additional Notifications

Add notifications for additional statuses, like “overhead tank level critically low”

Sync pump with government supply

Schedule the pump to fill the tank at the same time the government water supply is filling the sump

Personalize app to water usage

The user can tailor the water information to address specific uses, like cooking or gardening

Actionable alerts

Alerts could provide associated actions to address the issue in the alert

Set recurring pump filling

Automatically fill the tank using preset intervals scheduled by the user

Water bill payment

Instead of going to the bill office, pay the water bill through the app

Reflection

We only scratched the surface of what it would take to bring this solution to market.

This project allowed us to undergo the user- centered design process to better understand a problem in a non-American context. By researching and designing in a cultural context different from that of most of our group members, we realized the importance of always probing further and avoiding making assumptions. This led to some of our best and most interesting insights about water management systems. It reminded us of the value of remaining as objective as possible and letting users show and tell us what they needed. This was a valuable lesson we can take with us when we design for contexts that are more familiar to us..

User-centered design in another cultural context reminded us about the importance of avoiding assumptions.

As we continued through the project, we realized more and more that there are so many aspects of the product that we could do research and development on. We had to be very careful about scoping our project to something manageable in the time-frame of the class. Otherwise, the scope of work could have quickly escalated.
For example, we would have loved to understand the pricing and cost structure of the home devices, or created an on-boarding experience for our users. Finally, it would be interesting to understand how we would market this product to our users to adopt this technology.
