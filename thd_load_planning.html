Load Planning

Re-engineering the customer order loading process for efficient trailer loading and increased cost savings for the Home Depot.

ROLE

Product Designer

RESPONSIBILITIES

User Research, Stakeholder Alignment, Journey Mapping

TIMELINE

Jun 2019 - Present

Context

Customer orders that are picked within the DFCs are loaded into trailers which then take the items to the next distribution center for last-mile delivery. The process of planning how a trailer is supposed to be loaded is called ‘Load Planning’.

The Problem

Trailer loading was not a planned activity. The loaders were loading trailers in the FIFO order based on their best guess. As a result:

1. Customers orders getting split. 2. Trailer volume was not being efficiently used which impacted how many orders could fit inside a trailer.

Along with this, the carrier contracts were fixed and the Home Depot could not choose carriers based on the volume resulting in higher freight spend per lane.

Both of these issues was costing the company almost $2M/year.

The Goal

The goal was to help the DFC by planning the customer orders in advance before releasing the orders to the DFC for picking and loading the orders.

How might we better plan customer order loading within the trailers and allow for flexible carrier selection so that customer orders are delivered on time?

Prioritization

I facilitated a kick-off meeting with the stakeholders and led them through a goals-generation and prioritization exercise. The outcome was a prioritized list of goals based on what initiative added the most value to the company with least amount of complexity.

Prioritizing the top company goals and needs through a 2x2 exercise

Hypothesis

“By planning the trailer load in advance for the DFC Outbound operations, we will be able to:
1. Reduce/Eliminate customer orders getting split resulting in better customer experience.
2. Choose carriers based on volume resulting in cost savings for the company.
We’ll know this to be true when we see a reduction in orders being split and carriers being tendered based on volume demands.

Validation

I conducted interviews with various stakeholders to validated the hypothesis and assumptions and also surface any unforeseen issues.

Sythesizing the research interview data to identify themes around goals, risks, fears and opportunities.

Research Insights

The interviews helped us gain a deep understanding of the way the DFCs currently operate, opportunities to make changes and the risks involved in changing the current process.

Inefficient cubing was expensive

Due to lack of visibility to customer orders in advance, trailer cubing (stack items inside a trailer) was not efficient resulting in wasted trailer space.

Fixed carrier contracts

Fixed carrier contracts were resulting in customer delays since ship-schedule days were not on consecutive days.

Advance notice was critical

The DFCs had to start working on a trailer at least 4-5 hours before the scheduled departure time.

Re-printing labels very disruptive

If the carrier details changed after labels were printed, re-printing them is a very tedious process.

The MVP

Over the next few weeks, I worked with the business owners, Supply chain engineers and developers to ideate and brainstorm various solutions and strategies to address the top identified problems.
The MVP was to re-engineer the backend logic of handling customer so that orders could be virtually planned before releasing the orders to the DFCs.

Brainstorming

As a designer, my primary job was to help the team ask the right questions and create alignment within the team. I used various tactics and methods to help the team articulate and vizualize the ideas they had to solve the problem.

Flow Chart

The flow chart helped us understand the flow of a customer order through various systems and the different conditions that would trigger events within the system.

The data flow chart for the two shipping modes that shows dependencies and ownership by different teams.

Journey Map

The journey map helped us understand the journey of a customer order in detail, the various triggers within the journey, the different systems and touch-points within the process and everything that can go wrong (risk).
Understanding the entire journey was the most complex part of the process and in order help the team get a clear understanding, I used different ways to visualize the journey. In the end, the animated video turned out to be the best way to articulate the solution hypothesis.

The first attempt to understand the journey of a customer order through the backend systems.

Visualizing the journey through sketching.

Challenges

While the team had some sense of clarity on what the MVP and the next steps were, we were having difficulty in communicating and articulating the idea clearly within ourselves or to the other stakeholders owing to the complexity of the solution.

Also, The way customer orders were handled and assigned into virtual trailers depended on various scenarios and conditions and it was hard to visualize them on a static journey map.

Solution

To make it easier for the team to understand and share the MVP idea easily and facilitate better conversations, I created an animated video to explain the goals and the hypothesis.

The animated video to get a better understanding of the journey and the various cases and scenarios within the journey.

Outcomes

While the Load Planning project itself is a huge effort which takes a lot longer to see the impact on the users and the business, from a design perspective, the various activities I facilitated and the artefacts helped the team gain better clarity on the hypothesis.

Strong alignment

Strong alignment on the solution (hypothesis) between the Business and the Developers.

Less confusion

Less ambiguity and confusions within the team.

Increased clarity

Increased clarity on the problem and the proposed solution.

Better communication

Better conversations between the stakeholders.
